Turtle Population in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka is home to several species of turtles, including the Olive Ridley, Green Turtle, Hawksbill Turtle, and Leatherback Turtle. However, due to a number of factors including habitat loss, poaching, and pollution, all of these turtle species are in danger of going extinct.

Several marine turtle conservation projects have been established by Sri Lanka's Department of Wildlife Conservation in significant nesting locales across the nation. Despite these efforts, Sri Lanka's turtle populations are still in danger, necessitating additional work to ensure their survival.

History of Sea Turtles

Sea turtles have a rich history that spans millions of years. These ancient creatures have inhabited the Earth's oceans for over 100 million years, evolving into remarkable marine reptiles. Sea turtles have navigated the seas during various geological periods, adapting to changing environments and enduring significant challenges.

Unfortunately, human activities, such as habitat destruction, pollution, climate change, and poaching, have posed significant threats to sea turtles' survival. Conservation efforts and awareness campaigns have been crucial in safeguarding these magnificent creatures, aiming to protect their nesting grounds, reduce threats, and promote responsible fishing practices. We strive to secure a brighter future for sea turtles, ensuring their continued presence and contribution to the biodiversity and ecological health of our oceans.





Incredible Facts about Turtles


Sea Turtle Nesting: The Art of Egg Laying on Sandy Shores

Sea turtles rely on their rear flippers to excavate a cavity in the sandy beach, carefully crafting a nest in which they can safely deposit their eggs. This intricate process involves using their powerful flippers to dig into the sand, creating a suitable environment for the development of their offspring.

Hawksbill Turtles: Beaks Designed for Successful Prey Capture

Hawksbill turtles, scientifically known as Eretmochelys imbricata, possess uniquely adapted beaks that play a crucial role in their feeding behavior and prey selection. These turtles have developed specialized beaks composed of strong and durable keratin, allowing them to effectively capture and consume their preferred prey.

Adaptive Diving Abilities: Exploring the Underwater World of Sea Turtles

Sea turtles exhibit remarkable diving abilities, allowing them to spend extended periods submerged beneath the ocean's surface. These marine reptiles have evolved specialized adaptations that enable them to navigate and thrive in underwater environments.

The Unique Shell Adaptations of Sea Turtles

Sea turtles possess a distinctive physical characteristic: unlike other turtles, they cannot retreat or hide completely within their shells for protection. This inability to conceal themselves inside their shells sets sea turtles apart and highlights their remarkable adaptations for survival in their marine habitats.

Sea Turtle Categories

In Sri Lanka, the coastal waters are home to a diverse range of sea turtle species, including the Green Turtle, Hawksbill Turtle, Loggerhead Turtle, Leatherback Turtle, and Olive Ridley Turtle. These majestic creatures are an integral part of the coastal ecosystem, contributing to its balance and biodiversity.

Sri Lanka has recognized the importance of conserving these sea turtles and has implemented various initiatives and protective measures to ensure their survival. Conservation efforts, such as nesting beach monitoring, hatchery management, and public awareness campaigns, are in place to safeguard these endangered species and promote their sustainable coexistence with human activities along the coastline.

Turtle Hatcheries in Sri Laanka

  1. Hikkaduwa Turtle Conservation: Located in Hikkaduwa, this sanctuary focuses on the rescue, rehabilitation, and conservation of sea turtles, as well as raising awareness about their importance in the ecosystem.
  2. Kosgoda Turtle Conservation: Located in Kosgoda, this sanctuary focuses on the conservation of sea turtles, especially the endangered Loggerhead and Leatherback turtles.
  3. Rekawa Turtle Conservation: Situated in Rekawa, this project aims to protect nesting grounds and conserve sea turtle populations, including the Olive Ridley turtles that come to lay eggs on the beaches.
  4. Bentota Turtle Hatchery: Located in Bentota, this hatchery works towards the conservation of sea turtles, providing a safe environment for hatchlings and raising awareness about their protection.
  5. Peraliya Sea Turtle Conservation: Situated in Peraliya, this sanctuary focuses on conserving sea turtle species, including the Hawksbill and Green turtles, through research, education, and community involvement.

Life Cycle Of A Sea Turtle

Sea turtles undergo a remarkable life cycle involving distinct stages. During nesting season, female turtles come ashore to dig nests and lay eggs, after which they return to feeding areas to replenish their energy. Hatchlings emerge from the eggs, make their way to the ocean under the cover of darkness, and embark on a swimming frenzy to reach deeper waters. They then enter an open-ocean stage, living in flotsam and feeding on various resources for several years to decades.

After the oceanic phase, juvenile turtles move to nearshore feeding areas to continue their growth. Adult turtles also occupy these areas until they gather enough energy reserves to migrate to breeding areas for reproduction. Males and females migrate to coastal areas near nesting beaches to mate. Females lay multiple clutches of eggs, often returning to the same beach they hatched from (natal homing). Mating can involve multiple partners, as males actively seek out as many females as possible. Once mating is complete, males return to feeding areas, while females stay near the nesting beach during the nesting season.
